PhD claim visualisation

Victim-asymmetric denial impact

This page animates the hypothetical scenario presented in Mark Dorsett's PhD thesis. It is designed to make one central claim visible: the same denial mechanism can impose radically different economic harm depending on whether the victim is a small business or a large digital platform.

Core argument: availability attacks and sustainability attacks are not uniformly severe. Sustainability attacks can be disproportionately harmful to small organisations because metered infrastructure costs accumulate against limited annual profit. Availability attacks can be disproportionately harmful to large organisations because service interruption can disrupt high-volume digital revenue at scale.

Formal notation used in the thesis

Relative impact formulation

The visualisation below backs up Mark Dorsett's PhD claim by converting the thesis notation into a running graph. The graph can be viewed either as relative impact or as direct financial impact.

Attack--victim combinations

  • SA_sb — Sustainability attack against a small business
  • AA_sb — Availability attack against a small business
  • SA_lb — Sustainability attack against a large business
  • AA_lb — Availability attack against a large business

Relative impact ratio

I(t) = C(t) / P

C(t) is cumulative cost imposed by the attack at time t. P is annual profit or baseline financial capacity. This allows comparison across organisations of different scale.

View LaTeX notation used for this visualisation 
Let the following notation represent the four attack--victim combinations:

\begin{align}
SA_{sb} &:& \text{Sustainability attack against a small business} \\
AA_{sb} &:& \text{Availability attack against a small business} \\
SA_{lb} &:& \text{Sustainability attack against a large business} \\
AA_{lb} &:& \text{Availability attack against a large business}
\end{align}

Let $I(t)$ denote the \emph{relative impact} experienced by a victim
organisation at time $t$, defined as the ratio between the cumulative loss
generated by the attack and the baseline economic capacity of the organisation:

\begin{equation}
I(t) = \frac{C(t)}{P}
\end{equation}

What the animation shows

The same attack cost does not mean the same victim impact

Small businesses

For a small business, the sustainability attack rises sharply because metered cloud, serverless, or AI costs are large relative to the organisation's annual profit. This is the core reason DoW, EDoS, DDoW, and AI-resource exhaustion can be financially dangerous even when the business is not a major digital platform.

Large businesses

For a large organisation, the same sustainability cost is absorbed by a far larger profit base. However, an availability outage can interrupt high-volume digital transactions, producing a much steeper relative impact trajectory.

Educational scope: this page is a visual reproduction of a hypothetical research scenario. It does not model every industry, provider, or organisation. It explains why denial-attack impact is victim-asymmetric.

Why this matters

Denial severity depends on victim economics

These examples reinforce the central claim developed throughout Mark Dorsett's PhD work: denial attacks should not be treated as a uniform threat category. The relative severity of a denial attack emerges from the interaction between the attack mechanism and the victim's economic profile.

Availability-oriented denial strategies primarily target operational disruption. Sustainability-oriented attacks target the economic foundations of service delivery by forcing metered infrastructure, serverless, or AI-resource consumption. Effective denial resilience must therefore consider both technical attack mechanics and financial exposure.
